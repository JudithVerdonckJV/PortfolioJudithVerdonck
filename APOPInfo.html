A Piece Of Planet

Groupproject

The project

One of the courses at DAE puts us together with four to five other students in order to make a game. We get nothing except deadlines and a very wide restriction (like "co-op", "twinstick", ...), the rest is up to us.
Our group consisted of two programmers, counting myself, and three artists:

  • Myself; Judith Verdonck, programmer
  • Tom Kalders, programmer
  • Tatiana Devos, artist
  • Lars Korden, artist
  • María Martínez, artist
After brainstorming, we had a good grasp on what we wanted to do, and we were immediatly faced with a few coding challenges in the very foundation of our game mechanics, most of which I tackled.
This project was not my first time using Unreal Engine, but it was the first time using c++ in Unreal. It made for a slow start, but we quickly adapted.

Contribution

Programming

The first problem we had to solve, was the gravity. Unreal's gravity points down over the z-axis, while we had a spherical world. This meant we had to have gravity pointing to an arbitrary point. The gravity I made is not physically correct - there is no acceleration for example - because in this game, that kind of accuracy was not needed.
The second, way bigger problem, was making our planet consist out of destuctible tiles, each with their own bit of code. In other words, in order to not have to painstakingly place all of the tiles by hand, they somehow needed to be generated in place. While the code that solved this particular problem isn't complex, there was quite a bit of research and testing involved.

My other responsibilities were making all the enemy classes and getting the animation blueprints up and running for everything, as I was the only one with any experience with Unreal's animation system. In the end, I also did part of the UI (mostly the menu's), as we were running out of time.

Design

Coming up with the idea for the game was something we did in group, but as I took up the lead, I was often the one to either spark discussion, or make final decisions. An example of this would be the destructible tiles. As we were working in a rather small playfield (after settling on the planet-theme), I thought it would be nice if this environment itself would be a game mechanics factor. Someone came up with the idea of biomes, which ended up as the Tundra, Forest and Desert tiles, and I came up with the idea of having the planet be destructible. Of course, it is not as clear cut as that - these ideas grow very organically and often receive input and changes from all sides. Still, the base comes from somewhere, and I can confidently say I have played a big role in how that base was build up.

Other

I took up the responsibility of writing the weekly devlogs. While it sometimes was hard to properly word what the artists had done, as I had a less complete view on their struggles and tests, I had a lot of fun telling all the ups and downs we went through as a team.
